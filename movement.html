Observation. And the problem.

The people making the biggest decisions in business are also the least likely to admit they're struggling. Not because they're stronger than everyone else. Because the system punishes honesty and rewards performance. So they carry it quietly: the depression, the anxiety, the PTSD, the burnout that crosses the line into something clinical. And they never say a word, because saying it out loud could cost them the career they've spent decades building.

The Origin

I'll go first.

I'm Richard Olson. I've delivered over $200 million in documented business impact. I led intelligence operations during Operation Enduring Freedom. I built analytics platforms at the world's largest company. I grew a small business from $750K to $5M+ revenue by building the systems it never had.

In November 2023, I was hospitalized for major depression and PTSD.

Around the same time, a nine-year chapter at one of the world's largest companies came to an end. There are things about how that chapter ended that I'm not able to discuss. Not because I'm protecting anyone, but because legal agreements drew a line around the story. What I can tell you is this: I gave nine years. I built things that mattered. And when I needed the system to have room for someone who was struggling, it didn't.

I studied Russian film in college. Soviet directors spent careers learning to tell the truth under censorship: allegory, subtext, the weight of what isn't said. I never imagined I'd need those techniques to tell my own story on LinkedIn. But here I am.

Seven months later, I tried to go back. June 2024. I thought I was ready. I wasn't.

Thirty days in, someone told me my “personality doesn't fit.” Not that my work was wrong. Not that my analysis was flawed. My personality. That's not feedback you can act on. That's a label. And when you're still carrying the weight of a hospitalization, a career ending, and a story you're not allowed to tell, a label like that doesn't land on solid ground. It lands on a fracture.

I own my part. I went back too soon. I was functioning. Showing up, producing, solving problems. But I wasn't whole yet. And when you're not whole, you can't always control how you show up in a room. That's not an excuse. It's something worth understanding.

I could tell all of this as a grievance. But that's not the whole truth.

The whole truth is: large organizations aren't families. They're systems. Systems optimize for continuity. The people who reached out afterward weren't “the company.” They were individuals. The company is an abstraction. The people are real.

What came next wasn't a strategy. It was a surrender.

I stopped trying to perform my way out of it. I got radically transparent with myself about what I'd lost, what I'd contributed to, and what I couldn't fix alone. And I humbled myself enough to practice faith. Not faith as a platitude. Faith as a daily discipline. The willingness to believe that what I'm building now matters, even when I can't see where it leads.

Those two things, radical honesty and practiced faith, aren't the kind of tools that show up on a resume. But they're the reason I'm still building. They gave me the foundation to start again when the professional playbook had nothing left to offer.

In 2025, I started learning AI. Really learning it. And it became the tool that helped me rebuild. It gave me structure when I couldn't find my own. It gave me a way forward when my brain wouldn't cooperate.

I'm not sharing this because it's comfortable. I'm sharing it because the silence isn't helping anyone.

When I was in the worst of it, I looked for senior professionals who had been through the same thing and come out the other side. I couldn't find them. Not because they didn't exist, but because none of them were talking. That silence nearly cost me everything.

Not everyone goes through this. Some people have fortunate careers without stigmatizing setbacks, and I don't want to pretend otherwise. But I believe most professionals don't share their setbacks with the same honesty they share their successes. That gap costs all of us something. It costs the person carrying it in silence. And it costs the next person who goes through it alone because they never saw someone come out the other side.

I haven't proven anything yet. I'm testing a hypothesis: that radical transparency about where I've been, combined with demonstrated competence in what I can build, will open more doors than it closes. That the right organizations will see resilience as a qualification, not a risk.

I could be wrong. But I'd rather test it in public than wonder in private.

Right now, I'm in an active job search for senior leadership roles. And yes, I'm aware that hiring managers will read this page. That's by design. I'd rather you know who I am before the first conversation than manage your perception through the third. The setbacks I've described here are real, they're processed, and they inform how I lead, not whether I can. If anything, what I've been through has made me a more effective operator: slower to react, faster to listen, and unwilling to let the people around me suffer in silence the way I did.

And I'm documenting all of it because the space between “senior leader looking for a role” and “senior leader landing a role” is a silence that helps no one.

That's what #CarriedThat is for. Not a claim that everyone carries something. An invitation for those who do to stop carrying it alone.

#CarriedThat

This movement is for anyone carrying something they haven't said out loud.

Mental health. Career setbacks. The gap on the resume you can't explain in 30 seconds. Whatever it is, it shaped you. And it deserves more than silence.

🧠

The Rules

I reserve the right to tell the truth, the whole truth, or the parts that matter.

  1. Everything I share will be true. No embellishments, no invented metrics, no fabricated timelines. If I say a number, I can back it up.
  2. I will name what I carried. Depression. PTSD. Hospitalization. By name. Not because it's comfortable, but because euphemisms let everyone pretend it's not happening.
  3. I will not name people or companies in negative contexts. This is about destigmatizing setbacks, not settling scores. The point is what I carried, not who handed it to me.
  4. I will own my part. I wasn't always the hero. I didn't always handle it well. Accountability is the spine of this. Without it, vulnerability becomes performance.
  5. I will not perform recovery. I'm not “healed.” I'm not “past it.” I'm building. Some days are good. Some aren't. That's the truth and I won't polish it.
  6. I accept the risk. Some hiring managers will read this and pass. Some will read this and reach out. I'd rather work for the second kind.
The Fine Print (That Isn't Fine Print)

This isn't a safety blanket.

I'm not going to promise you that sharing your story will be safe. Some people will use your honesty against you. Some hiring managers will pass on your resume because you admitted to being human. Some colleagues will treat you differently.

That's real. I won't pretend otherwise.

But I will tell you this:

I cannot prevent someone from discriminating against you for your honesty. But do you really want their approval?

The people who reach out because of your story are the people worth building with. The doors that close because you told the truth were never your doors. That's not optimism. That's a filtering mechanism. And for what it's worth, the DMs I've received since I started sharing have been from executives, veterans, founders, and leaders who said: “Thank you. I carry something too.”
