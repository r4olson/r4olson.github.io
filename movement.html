The Manifesto

I reserve the right to tell the truth, the whole truth, or the parts that matter.

  1. Everything I share will be true. No embellishments, no invented metrics, no fabricated timelines. If I say a number, I can back it up.
  2. I will not name companies or individuals in negative contexts. This is about my journey, not settling scores. If you recognize a story, that's your context — not my accusation.
  3. I will share things most career advisors would tell me not to. Hospitalizations. Firings. The gap between the LinkedIn highlight reel and the actual experience. Not because shock value — because silence helps no one.
  4. I will track and share my metrics in real time. Impressions, connections, meetings booked, applications sent, rejections received. If I'm going to tell others to be data-driven, I should go first.
  5. I will admit when I don't know. I'm learning AI alongside implementing it. I'm rebuilding alongside building. The uncertainty is the content.
  6. I will not perform vulnerability. There's a difference between being honest and being strategic about honesty. I'm doing both, and I'll tell you when I'm doing both.
  7. I accept the risk. Some hiring managers will read this and pass. Some will read this and reach out. I'd rather work for the second kind.

The Origin

Where this started.

I was fired. Twice. Eight months apart. After twenty years of building things — in military intelligence, at a Fortune 1 company, in my own business.

The first time, someone told me my "personality doesn't fit." Not that my work was wrong. Not that my analysis was flawed. My personality.

There's a difference between feedback and a label. Feedback sounds like: "When you did X, the impact was Y." You can work with that. You can improve. A label sounds like: "Change who you are." You can't work with that. You can only carry it.

I can't prevent someone from discriminating against you for your honesty. But do you really want their approval?

In November 2023, I was hospitalized for major depression and PTSD. Around the same time, a nine-year chapter at one of the world's largest companies came to an end.

I could tell this story as a grievance. But that's not the whole truth.

The whole truth is: I had been running on fumes for longer than I admitted. The organization didn't break me. I had been breaking myself, and I didn't ask for help until it was too late.

Large organizations aren't families. They're systems. Systems optimize for continuity. The people who reached out afterward weren't "the company." They were individuals. The company is an abstraction. The people are real.

I studied Russian film in college. Soviet directors learned to tell the truth sideways — allegory, subtext, the fig in the pocket. I never thought I'd use those lessons on LinkedIn.

I'm trying a third option: tell the truth, take what's mine, and build something better. — The Fortune 1 Story, Week 4

In 2025, I started learning AI — really learning it — and it became the tool that helped me rebuild. It gave me structure when I couldn't find my own. Now I'm documenting the entire journey: the strategies, the metrics, the setbacks, the rebuilding. Not because I have it figured out. Because the silence between "executive looking for a role" and "executive landing a role" helps no one.

#CarriedThat isn't about being a victim. It's about being honest about the weight. Every successful person you admire has a story they haven't told publicly — feedback that wounded them, an ending they didn't choose, a label they couldn't shake. This movement gives them a format to share it, on their terms.

The Fine Print (That Isn't Fine Print)

This isn't a safety blanket.

Most movements tell you that sharing your story will be rewarded. That everyone will clap. That vulnerability is always met with warmth.

I'm not going to tell you that. Some people will use your honesty against you. Some hiring managers will pass on your resume because you admitted to being human. Some colleagues will judge.

And here's what I believe about that:

I cannot prevent someone from discriminating against you for your honesty. But do you really want their approval?

The people who reach out because of your story — those are the people worth building with. The doors that close because you told the truth were never your doors. That's not optimism. That's a filtering mechanism.
